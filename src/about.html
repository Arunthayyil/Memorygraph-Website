Photography is just listening with a camera — being patient enough to be in the right place when the moment decides to exist.

01
He'll be there. Personally.

When you book Memorygraph, you book Arun. Not an associate, not whoever is available that weekend. He leads every wedding we take from the first conversation to the final delivered gallery. That is not something we're willing to change as we grow.

02
He knows Kerala weddings.

We grew up with these ceremonies. We know when the muhurtham is. We know to step back during the Nischayam and follow the thaali through the crowd. Nothing needs explaining. Nothing will be missed because we didn't understand it. We consider that a responsibility, not just a privilege.

03
He'll disappear into the room.

The real moments only surface when people stop performing for the camera. We've spent years learning to be invisible — not absent, but unobtrusive. To read a room so well we're simply part of it. We wait for that. We're very patient.

Our philosophy

The best photographs happen
when the photographer disappears.

01
Presence over direction

We rarely ask couples to pose. Not because we can't — because the real photographs come from real moments. There are always more of them than people expect. We just have to be patient enough to wait for them.

02
Light as language

Kerala's golden hour is unlike anywhere else. We plan every wedding around it — arrive before the light does, stay after it leaves. Light is the difference between a photograph and a document.

03
Editing as storytelling

Post-production is the final act of the story. The grade that decides whether a frame feels like that morning or just any morning. Warm, cinematic, restrained. Designed to age the way good photographs age.

How we got here

The story
so far.

Childhood · Thrissur
A camera before I knew what it meant

My sister and her husband gifted me a camera when I was young. I'd go on long walks and come back with photographs — no plan, no technique. Just a genuine feeling for looking at the world a certain way. That feeling never left.

The year everything broke
Infopark, then the forest

Electronics diploma. Campus placement at an MNC in Kochi. One year, then I resigned — withdrew my PF savings and bought a professional camera. Spent the next months alone in forests learning patience and stillness. Both turned out to matter.

∼2014 · First wedding
A morning in Kottayam I still remember

A friend's cousin needed a photographer and I said yes reluctantly — I'd never photographed people. I made technical mistakes. But the photographs were real, and I realised I wasn't bad at this at all. That morning changed everything.

2020 · The studio
Memorygraph

Founded with one principle: only work we're genuinely proud of, with couples we genuinely connect with. Small by design. The early people who believed before the portfolio existed — they built this with us.

I did a diploma in electronics. Got a campus placement at an MNC in Infopark, Kochi — the kind of job that looked good on paper and felt completely wrong from the inside. I lasted a year. Not because I wasn't trying, but because I was struggling with everything — the work, the environment, the version of myself I was becoming. I was at the brink of something I didn't want to name at the time. So I resigned.

Photography wasn't a plan. It was the one thing that had always made sense. When I was young, my sister — seven years older than me — and her husband gifted me a camera. I'd go on walks and come back with photographs. I had no idea what I was doing technically, but I had a genuine feeling for it. That never went away.

After resigning, I withdrew my PF fund and borrowed a little from my father to buy my first professional camera. Then I spent months alone in forests. Wildlife photography — birds, mostly. A paradise flycatcher once. Snakes sometimes. It was quiet work, solitary work, and I was good at being patient and invisible. Those two things turned out to matter a lot later.

The first wedding came through a friend. His cousin was getting married in Kottayam, budget was tight, and he thought of me. I said I wasn't sure I could photograph people — all my work until then had been birds and animals. He insisted. I went.

I still remember every moment of that wedding. I can picture the light in that room, the specific nervousness of not knowing how to move in a crowd with a camera. I made technical mistakes. But looking at those photographs now, I still like them. There's something in them that was real. That morning I understood I wasn't bad at photographing people at all. I'd just never tried.

What followed was a few years of building slowly. Small functions, engagements, a second wedding that was a friend's own. Early on, the people who believed in me did so without a large portfolio to justify it. I've never forgotten that. Those early supporters are the reason Memorygraph exists.
