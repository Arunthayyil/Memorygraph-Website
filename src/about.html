"There's a moment at every wedding — usually when nobody's looking — where something true happens. That's the only frame I care about getting right."

Arun grew up in Thrissur — his first camera was a gift from his sister, long before he understood what photography could become. After a year working at an MNC in Infopark, Kochi, he resigned, withdrew his PF savings, and bought a professional camera. He spent the next year alone in forests, learning patience and stillness — the two things that turned out to matter most in a wedding. A friend's cousin's wedding in Kottayam confirmed it.

I did a diploma in electronics. Got a campus placement at an MNC in Infopark, Kochi — the kind of job that looked good on paper and felt completely wrong from the inside. I lasted a year. Not because I wasn't trying, but because I was struggling with everything — the work, the environment, the version of myself I was becoming. I was at the brink of something I didn't want to name at the time. So I resigned.

Photography wasn't a plan. It was the one thing that had always made sense. When I was young, my sister — seven years older than me — and her husband gifted me a camera. I'd go on walks and come back with photographs. I had no idea what I was doing technically, but I had a genuine feeling for it. That never went away.

After resigning, I withdrew my PF fund and borrowed a little from my father to buy my first professional camera. Then I spent months alone in forests. Wildlife photography — birds, mostly. A paradise flycatcher once. Snakes sometimes. It was quiet work, solitary work, and I was good at being patient and invisible. Those two things turned out to matter a lot later.

The first wedding came through a friend. His cousin was getting married in Kottayam, budget was tight, and he thought of me. I said I wasn't sure I could photograph people — all my work until then had been birds and animals. He insisted. I went.

I still remember every moment of that wedding. It's been roughly ten years and I can picture the light in that room, the specific nervousness of not knowing how to move in a crowd with a camera. I made technical mistakes — shutter speed off, ISO wrong in places. But looking at those photographs now, I still like them. There's something in them that was real. That morning I understood I wasn't bad at photographing people at all. I'd just never tried.

What followed was a few years of building slowly. Small functions, engagements, a second wedding that was a friend's own. Early on, the people who believed in me did so without a large portfolio to justify it. I've never forgotten that. Those early supporters are the reason Memorygraph exists.

Our Philosophy

Photography is just
listening
with a camera.

The best wedding photographs don't happen because a photographer gave perfect directions. They happen when a photographer disappears into the room, reads the energy, and is present in exactly the right moment.

We've spent years learning to be invisible — not absent, but unobtrusive. To blend into a celebration so completely that people forget we're there. That's when the real moments surface. And those are always the ones that mean the most, years later.

Kerala weddings carry a particular depth — the rituals, the golden light, the generations gathered in one space. We grew up with these ceremonies. Nothing needs explaining to us, and nothing will be missed because we didn't understand it. We consider that a responsibility, not just a privilege.
