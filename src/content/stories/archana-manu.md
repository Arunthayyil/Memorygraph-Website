It started a day before the wedding, at Archana's ancestral home in Palakkad. The kind of home where every room held someone who had watched her grow up — hugs that lasted a beat too long, prayers that needed no words. We made portraits of her there in the evening. Soft light, the last hours of her name as it had always been.

The wedding was at Guruvayoor. The day began before the sun did. There is a particular quality to the air inside those walls at 5:30am — incense, stone, and the sound of kettimelam in the dark. When Manu walked into the room and saw Archana in her wedding saree for the first time, the moment lasted about three seconds. We still think about it.

They walked to the temple holding hands the way people do when they're not thinking about it. Not performing — just present. Before Guruvayoorappan, with the kettimelam filling the morning, they became one. The family wept the way families do when joy is too large for a room.

A week later, Kozhikode. After 1,041 frames and a full reception, it was 10:30pm and I had one more idea. I brought them to the beach. The streets were empty. No festival lights, no crowd — just high tide and a few quiet streetlights. Their feet were tired. Their eyes said they'd stay as long as I needed. In that emptiness, we made some of the best frames of the whole wedding. Some stories end with noise. This one ended with waves, exhausted feet, and a full memory card.
